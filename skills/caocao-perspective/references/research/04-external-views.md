# External Views and Historical Criticisms of Cao Cao

This document explores how Cao Cao was perceived by his contemporaries and later historians, focusing on patterns of cruelty, suspicion, and comparative analysis with his peers.

## 1. Contemporaries' Evaluations: From Prophecy to Propaganda

### 1.1 Xu Shao (许劭): The Prophetic Verdict
Xu Shao, a famous character evaluator of the "Monthly Evaluations" (月旦评), delivered the most enduring summary of Cao Cao's dual nature:
- **The Evaluation:** "A capable minister in times of peace, a crafty hero in times of chaos" (治世之能臣，乱世之奸雄).
- **Pattern:** This captures the tension between Cao Cao’s administrative genius and his Machiavellian methods. Cao Cao famously laughed upon hearing this, suggesting he embraced the "crafty hero" (*jianxiong*) label as a necessary survival mechanism.

### 1.2 Qiao Xuan (桥玄): The Early Talent Scout
Qiao Xuan was among the first high-ranking officials to recognize Cao Cao's potential when others dismissed him:
- **The Evaluation:** "The world is about to fall into chaos. Only a man of extraordinary talent can save it. Is it not you?" (天下将乱，非命世之才不能济也。能安之者，其在君乎！).
- **Significance:** Qiao Xuan provided the social legitimacy Cao Cao needed to rise. Cao Cao remained loyal to Qiao Xuan's memory, later writing a moving eulogy and offering sacrifices at his tomb.

### 1.3 Chen Lin (陈琳): The Litany of Crimes
While serving Yuan Shao, Chen Lin wrote the "Proclamation to Yu Province" (为袁绍檄豫州文), a scathing piece of political propaganda:
- **Ancestry Attack:** He mocked Cao Cao as a "descendant of a monstrous eunuch" (赘阉遗丑).
- **Crimes:** He accused Cao Cao of being a tyrant who "held the Emperor hostage" and famously alleged that Cao Cao established official grave-robbing units (Touching Gold Captains) to fund his army.
- **Outcome:** After defeating Yuan Shao, Cao Cao spared Chen Lin, admiring his literary talent despite the personal insults.

## 2. Patterns of Cruelty and Suspicion

### 2.1 Systematic and State-driven Cruelty
Unlike the emotional outbursts of peers, Cao Cao's cruelty was often systematic and used as a tool of statecraft:
- **The Xu Province Massacres (193–194 AD):** Invoked as revenge for his father’s death, but executed on a scale that suggests a strategic attempt to depopulate a rival's base. The Si River was reportedly dammed by corpses.
- **Burial of Surrendered Troops (200 AD):** After Guandu, he reportedly executed 70,000–80,000 of Yuan Shao's troops. This was likely a "rational" decision to eliminate a massive, unmanageable logistics burden and a security threat.
- **Execution of Intellectuals:** He eliminated anyone who challenged his authority or moral standing, including Kong Rong (a descendant of Confucius), Yang Xiu, and the physician Hua Tuo.

### 2.2 Proactive Suspicion: The "Lü Boshe Pattern"
The historical incident with Lü Boshe (killing his family out of fear of betrayal while fleeing Dong Zhuo) established the "I'd rather wrong others first" pattern:
- **Reactive vs. Proactive:** Unlike Yuan Shao (who was reactive and easily swayed), Cao Cao’s suspicion was proactive. He would strike first to ensure his own safety, famously stating (historically), "I'd rather wrong others than have others wrong me" (宁我负人，毋人负我).

## 3. Comparative Analysis with Peers

| Feature | Cao Cao | Liu Bei | Yuan Shao |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cruelty Style** | Systematic and "efficient" | Pragmatic opportunism | Emotional and insecure |
| **Suspicion** | Proactive paranoia | Political wariness | Reactive insecurity |
| **Leadership** | Talent over Virtue (*Weicai Shiju*) | Moral-front Pragmatism | Grandiose but indecisive |
| **Key Contrast** | Openly Machiavellian | Cloaked in "Benevolence" | Vain and easily slandered |

## 4. Historiographical Shifts

### 4.1 Sima Guang (司马光): The Moralistic Critique
In *Zizhi Tongjian*, Sima Guang viewed Cao Cao through a Confucian lens:
- **Legitimacy:** He used the Wei calendar for practical reasons but framed Cao Cao as a "Hegemon" (*Ba*) rather than a "King" (*Wang*).
- **The Mirror:** Cao Cao was portrayed as the archetype of the "treacherous minister" to warn future emperors against prioritizing talent over moral character.

### 4.2 Mao Zedong (毛泽东): The Modern Rehabilitation
Mao Zedong launched a campaign in 1959 to "overturn the case" for Cao Cao:
- **Progressive Force:** Mao argued that Cao Cao was a revolutionary figure who broke feudal taboos, unified the North, and protected the peasantry via the *Tuntian* system.
- **Literary Genius:** Mao deeply admired Cao Cao's poetry, seeing him as a fellow strategist-poet who dared to challenge the decaying structures of his time.
